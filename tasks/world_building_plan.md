# World Building Plan

The world of Opus Civile begins not with a story, but with a question: what kind
Of world would this actually be? Before any narrative can be written, the setting
Must be earned. This document lays out the work required to get there.

---

## Layer 1: Understanding the Real Industrial Revolution

Before we can reimagine it, we need to understand what it actually was — not
Just the famous inventions, but the underlying concepts and cascading effects
That made it transformative.

- [ ] Identify the key technologies of the real Industrial Revolution
- [ ] Identify the concepts and preconditions that enabled them
- [ ] Understand the social and economic effects that followed

---

## Layer 2: Understanding the Roman Republic

The revolution doesn't happen in a vacuum. It happens here, in this specific
World, and that world will shape everything about how it unfolds.

- [ ] Political structure — factions, Senate, tribunes, power dynamics
- [ ] Economic foundations — trade, slavery, land ownership, currency
- [ ] Military structure and its relationship to political power
- [ ] Social structure — classes, citizenship, the role of freed peoples
- [ ] Existing technology and engineering capability
- [ ] Geography — key cities, trade routes, regional identities
- [ ] The fault lines already present in the late Republic

---

## Layer 3: Filtering the Revolution Through Roman Constraints

With both worlds understood, we work through each technology and concept and
Ask: is this feasible given what Romans knew and could make? What form would it
Take? What simply cannot exist?

- [ ] Assess each technology against Roman metallurgy and materials
- [ ] Assess each technology against Roman chemistry and scientific understanding
- [ ] Identify workarounds or alternatives where direct translation fails
- [ ] Determine what is possible, partially possible, or impossible
- [ ] Note any technologies unique to the Roman world that might emerge instead

---

## Layer 4: Mapping the Effects

Once we know what exists, we work out what it actually does to Roman society.

- [ ] How does industrialization interact with the slave economy?
- [ ] How does it shift the balance between political factions?
- [ ] How does it affect military capacity and the business of war?
- [ ] How does it ripple through trade and the Mediterranean economy?
- [ ] Does it accelerate, prevent, or reshape the fall of the Republic?

---

## Layer 5: Building the Innovation Timeline

The revolution is a cascade, not an event. This layer maps how innovation
Spreads across the Republic — where it starts, how it travels, and what the
World looks like when our story begins.

- [ ] Establish where the revolution begins and why
- [ ] Map the spread of key innovations across regions and factions
- [ ] Build the pre-story timeline of what has already changed
- [ ] Identify what is still actively in flux when the narrative opens

---

## Layer 6: The Story

With the world built, the story can be written. The narrative grows from the
Setting rather than being imposed on it.

- [ ] Find the story's entry point into this world
- [ ] Develop key characters and their relationship to the revolution
- [ ] Outline the central conflict
- [ ] Write a standalone narrative, with an eye toward whether the world
  Supports something larger

---

*The world comes first. The stories will follow.*
